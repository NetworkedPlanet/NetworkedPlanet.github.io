Linked Data 101 - What's a predicate?

Feb 16, 2016 • Jen

In the first blog post in this series on Linked Data 101, we took a look at the use of URIs in linked data. We left off just as we were getting to a really interesting part - predicates!

Ever since the initial vision of the World Wide Web was dreamt of, the idea of being able to provide context and meaning to the information published has been part of that plan. Whilst it was laid to one side for the initial versions of the web, that goal has never been forgotten. When we look at two disparate pieces of information on the web, how do we decide that the two pieces have overlapping meanings, perhaps they are talking about different subjects, but they contain information relevant to each other. Currently we need to do that process manually, or have extremely complex linguistic programs to try and do the work for us.

With linked data (or as I like to think about it - linkable data) we explicitly describe the link between a Thing and its properties with a unique identifier, and what unique identifiers do we use on the web? You guessed it - URIs. Why the need for identifiers? Well, let’s say I have some information that contains details about Wards, depending on the context that information could be about rooms in a hospital, a division of a geographical area governed by councillors, or even a young person who has been appointed a guardian by a court. By using URI predicates we have a way of making these differences in meaning explicit.

Let’s look again at some simple tabular data and how it would translate into triples. 

ID,Event,Location - Lat,Location - Long,Date & Time
EV890,Introduction To Linked Data,53.3957985,-1.5694953,13/7/2016 11:00:00 AM
EV891,Query the Web of Data with SPARQL,53.2205365,-4.1601993,20/4/2016 7:00:00 PM
EV892,Better data means easier collaboration,55.8555367,-4.3024977,3/8/2016 6:30:00 PM

In RDF the triples would look something like this:

<http://example.org/events/EV890> <http://example.org/events/title> "Introduction To Linked Data" .
<http://example.org/events/EV890> <http://example.org/events/latitude> "53.3957985" .
<http://example.org/events/EV890> <http://example.org/events/longitude> "-1.5694953" .
<http://example.org/events/EV890> <http://example.org/events/date> "2016-07-13T11:00:00.0000000+00:00"^^<http://www.w3.org/2001/XMLSchema#dateTime> .
<http://example.org/events/EV891> <http://example.org/events/title> "Query the Web of Data with SPARQL" .
<http://example.org/events/EV891> <http://example.org/events/latitude> "53.2205365" .
<http://example.org/events/EV891> <http://example.org/events/longitude> "-4.1601993" .
<http://example.org/events/EV891> <http://example.org/events/date> "2016-04-20T19:00:00.0000000+00:00"^^<http://www.w3.org/2001/XMLSchema#dateTime> .
<http://example.org/events/EV892> <http://example.org/events/title> "Better data means easier collaboration" .
<http://example.org/events/EV892> <http://example.org/events/latitude> "55.8555367" .
<http://example.org/events/EV892> <http://example.org/events/longitude> "-4.3024977" .
<http://example.org/events/EV892> <http://example.org/events/date> "2016-08-03T18:30:00.0000000+00:00"^^<http://www.w3.org/2001/XMLSchema#dateTime> .

As it is the data would be fine to publish on your internal or external data portal (using your portal’s address rather than example.org of course!). It’s 4 star data, meaning that not only is it in a non-proprietary format but that you’ve used URIs to denote your resources. Your data is linkable, regardless of whether you choose to share it outside of your organisation. Rather than concentrating on linked data being about whether or not you choose to publish publicly, think of how you can make data that’s already out there easier to consume, and how easy it will be to merge it with your own data.

A vocabulary collects together sets of predicates that are useful in describing specific types of things (they can also contain more information about describing types and how types relate to one another but that’s beyond the scope of this article).

The most used vocabulary at this point in time is the Dublin Core vocabulary. This is a vocabulary published by the Dublin Core Metadata Initiative used to describe information about documents - such as Title, Description, Creator, and so on - so you can see why it would be such a well used vocabulary. Another useful vocabulary is from Geonames, containing properties for describing places such as Name, Alternative Name, Population and suchlike. It’s worthwhile to note that while Geonames has many properties for describing places, for latitude and longitude it reuses the W3C Basic Geo vocabulary.

A hugely popular vocabulary (due to its support in Google, Microsoft and Yahoo) is Schema.org. Many plugins for CMS website use the terms defined in Schema.org in order to embed the information in their webpages that they are talking about (for example) dates of events, and therefore when they appear on the search result they have additional useful information about upcoming events under their site name and description.

Using published vocabularies

To make use of a published vocabulary, simply replace your predicate with the predicate from the published vocabulary. I’ve chosen to use the Basic Geo Vocab to describe my latitude and longitude, and Schema.org to describe the other details of my event. This changes my RDF to:

<http://example.org/events/EV890> <http://schema.org/name> "Introduction To Linked Data" .
<http://example.org/events/EV890> <http://www.w3.org/2003/01/geo/wgs84_pos#lat> "53.3957985" .
<http://example.org/events/EV890> <http://www.w3.org/2003/01/geo/wgs84_pos#long> "-1.5694953" .
<http://example.org/events/EV890> <http://schema.org/startDate> "2016-07-13T11:00:00.0000000+00:00"^^<http://www.w3.org/2001/XMLSchema#dateTime> .
<http://example.org/events/EV891> <http://schema.org/name> "Query the Web of Data with SPARQL" .
<http://example.org/events/EV891> <http://www.w3.org/2003/01/geo/wgs84_pos#lat> "53.2205365" .
<http://example.org/events/EV891> <http://www.w3.org/2003/01/geo/wgs84_pos#long> "-4.1601993" .
<http://example.org/events/EV891> <http://schema.org/startDate> "2016-04-20T19:00:00.0000000+00:00"^^<http://www.w3.org/2001/XMLSchema#dateTime> .
<http://example.org/events/EV892> <http://schema.org/name> "Better data means easier collaboration" .
<http://example.org/events/EV892> <http://www.w3.org/2003/01/geo/wgs84_pos#lat> "55.8555367" .
<http://example.org/events/EV892> <http://www.w3.org/2003/01/geo/wgs84_pos#long> "-4.3024977" .
<http://example.org/events/EV892> <http://schema.org/startDate> "2016-08-03T18:30:00.0000000+00:00"^^<http://www.w3.org/2001/XMLSchema#dateTime> .

How to choose a vocab

It’s all very well for me to talk about how I chose to use a couple of popular vocabularies and then plonked them in my data, but how are you going to know about these vocabularies and which to use? Well, there are websites out there that help you find existing published vocabularies, such as LOV, another good starting point is to reach out to the publishers of the Open Data that you are consuming and ask them about their preferred vocabularies, and of course we would also suggest getting in touch with your friendly Linked Data experts to ask for advice too (yes, that is indeed a link to our contact detail page, it had to be done!).
